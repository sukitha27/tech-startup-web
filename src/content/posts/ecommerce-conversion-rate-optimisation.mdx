Conversion rate optimisation (CRO) has a noise problem. The internet is full of listicles claiming "37 ways to increase your e-commerce conversions" that bury the two things that actually matter under 35 things that don't.

After building and optimising over a dozen online stores, here's what we've consistently found moves the needle — and what's mostly noise.

## What Actually Works

### 1. Site Speed, Full Stop

This is the most consistently impactful change we make. A one-second improvement in page load time increases conversion rates by an average of 2–3% according to Google's own data. For stores doing $10,000/month, that's $200–300 in additional revenue per second of improvement. Per month.

The specific changes that deliver the most speed improvement:

**Image optimisation** is usually the biggest win. Most store owners upload full-resolution photos from their camera or phone. A product image that should be 80KB is being served at 4MB. Modern formats (WebP, AVIF) with proper sizing can cut image weight by 70–80%.

**Removing unused JavaScript.** The average e-commerce site loads 500KB+ of JavaScript. Much of it is from third-party widgets, chat tools, and analytics scripts that are loaded eagerly even on pages that don't use them. Loading these scripts asynchronously or deferring them can cut Time to Interactive by seconds.

**A proper CDN.** If your store is hosted on a single server in Singapore and your customers are in Europe, every resource travels across the planet on every page load. A CDN caches your assets globally. This is a hosting decision that costs $10–20/month and can improve load times by 40–60% for geographically distant customers.

### 2. Product Photography

We've seen stores with identical products double their conversion rate after replacing amateur photos with professional ones. This is not a web development problem — it's a business investment — but the impact is real and measurable.

Specifically: multiple angles, a clean/white background option, lifestyle shots showing the product in context, and for apparel, photos showing the product on a real person rather than a flat lay or mannequin.

### 3. The Checkout Flow

Cart abandonment averages 70% across e-commerce. A significant portion of that is recoverable with checkout optimisation:

**Guest checkout.** Forcing account creation before purchase is the single most common conversion killer we see. Offer it as an option, never a requirement.

**Showing total cost early.** Surprise shipping fees revealed at the final checkout step are the leading cause of abandonment. Show estimated shipping (or free shipping eligibility) on the product page and cart.

**Reducing form fields.** The average checkout form has 23 fields. Most can be reduced to 8–12 without losing necessary information. Every extra field is friction. Every friction point costs conversions.

**Payment options.** In 2025, a checkout that doesn't offer at minimum card, PayPal, and a buy-now-pay-later option (Klarna, Afterpay, etc.) is leaving money on the table. In Sri Lanka specifically, supporting local payment methods alongside international cards is essential.

### 4. Product Page Copy

Most product descriptions are either copied from the manufacturer (duplicate content, bad for SEO) or a bullet list of technical specs that tells the customer nothing about why they should buy.

Effective product copy answers: What problem does this solve? What will my life look like after I own this? What would I regret not knowing before I bought it?

This isn't about being flowery — it's about giving the customer the information they need to say yes with confidence.

### 5. Trust Signals

First-time visitors to your store don't know you. They're weighing the risk of handing over their credit card details to a stranger. Trust signals reduce that perceived risk:

- Genuine customer reviews with photos (not just star ratings)
- A clear, human-readable returns policy
- Security badges (SSL, payment processor logos)
- A real physical address and phone number
- Response time indicators ("We typically reply within 2 hours")

The most underrated trust signal: a phone number. Most customers won't call, but knowing they *can* call dramatically increases trust.

## What's Usually Noise

### Countdown Timers

"Only 3 left in stock! Sale ends in 14:32!" — if the timer resets every time you visit the page (and customers now know it does), you're training your customers to distrust you. Scarcity and urgency only convert when they're real.

### Pop-ups on Entry

An overlay asking for your email before you've read a single word of content is one of the most reliable ways to annoy new visitors. Exit-intent pop-ups with genuine offers (10% off your first order, not just "subscribe to our newsletter") are a different matter and can work.

### Complex Loyalty Programs at Small Scale

Loyalty programs make sense at scale. For stores doing under $50,000/month, the development cost and operational overhead of a loyalty program almost never pays back faster than the same investment in speed, photography, or checkout optimisation.

### Chatbots Pretending to be Humans

Automated chatbots that greet every visitor with "Hi! I'm Sarah, how can I help you today?" when Sarah is clearly a script undermine trust. A straightforward "Chat with us" button that connects to a real person (or honestly discloses it's automated) works better.

## A Practical Prioritisation

If you have limited time and budget, do these in order:

1. **Run Google PageSpeed Insights** on your homepage and best-selling product page. Fix anything in the red.
2. **Enable guest checkout** if you haven't already.
3. **Show total cost (including shipping) on the product page** or at minimum the cart, not the final checkout step.
4. **Get 10 genuine customer reviews with photos** on your top three products.
5. **Rewrite product descriptions** for your top five products with the customer's perspective in mind.

These five changes consistently outperform anything else we've tried.
