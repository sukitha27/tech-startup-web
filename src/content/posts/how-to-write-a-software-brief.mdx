We've received thousands of project enquiries over the years. The ones that result in accurate quotes, smooth projects, and happy clients all have one thing in common: the client came in with a clear brief.

The ones that result in budget overruns, scope creep, and frustration — on both sides — almost always started with some version of: "We need an app. Can you give us a quote?"

This guide will help you write a brief that gets you accurate quotes, protects your budget, and sets your project up for success.

## Why Vague Briefs Are Expensive

When a developer receives a vague brief, they do one of two things: they either quote conservatively high (to protect against unknown complexity), or they quote low and discover the real scope during development.

The first scenario means you're paying for risk you might not need to. The second means you'll face change requests and budget conversations mid-project — the most expensive and stressful time to discover a project is larger than expected.

A clear brief aligns everyone's understanding before money changes hands.

## What to Include in Your Brief

### 1. The Problem You're Solving

Not the solution — the problem. This sounds obvious but most briefs skip it.

**Vague:** "We need a booking system."
**Clear:** "Currently, customers call to book appointments. Our staff spends 3–4 hours per day managing bookings by phone and updating a spreadsheet. We want customers to be able to book online without staff involvement."

The second version tells a developer what they're actually building *for*, which affects every decision they make. A booking system for a two-person salon is architecturally very different from a booking system for a clinic with 20 practitioners.

### 2. Who Will Use It

Describe your users concretely:
- Who are they? (customers, staff, administrators, all three?)
- How technically comfortable are they?
- What devices will they primarily use? (mobile phone, desktop, tablet)
- Roughly how many users are you expecting at launch and in 12 months?

User scale affects infrastructure decisions significantly. A system serving 100 users internally is architecturally different from one serving 100,000 customers publicly.

### 3. Core Features (Must-Haves)

List the features without which the product doesn't work. Be specific:

- "Customers can view available appointment slots by day and book one"
- "Customers receive a confirmation email with their booking details"
- "Staff can view all upcoming bookings in a calendar view"
- "Staff can manually add, edit, or cancel bookings"
- "Customers receive a reminder SMS 24 hours before their appointment"

Each of these is a specific, testable feature. "A booking system" is not.

### 4. Nice-to-Have Features

These are features you'd like but don't need for launch. Being honest about this distinction is valuable — a developer can often include a nice-to-have for minimal additional cost if they know about it during architecture, but adding it retrospectively is always more expensive.

### 5. What You Don't Want

Constraints are as useful as requirements:

- "This must work without an internet connection" (offline-first requirement)
- "This must integrate with our existing Xero accounting system"
- "Users should never be shown a competitor's products"
- "This should not store any payment card details on our servers"

These constraints can dramatically affect technical approach and cost. Knowing them upfront prevents expensive rework.

### 6. Technical Constraints

Do you have existing systems it needs to connect to? An existing website, a CRM, an ERP system, a loyalty platform? Even if you don't know the technical details, naming them lets a developer ask the right questions.

Do you have hosting preferences or requirements? Some organisations require data to stay within a specific country due to compliance requirements.

### 7. Timeline

When do you actually need this? Is the deadline hard (a trade show, a legislative deadline, a contract requirement) or soft (we'd like to launch before the busy season)?

A hard deadline affects everything — scope, staffing, price. A soft deadline gives flexibility for a more measured approach.

### 8. Budget Range

This is the one most clients resist including, usually because they fear it anchors the quote. The opposite is true. Without a budget range, a developer doesn't know whether to propose the right solution, a premium solution, or a minimum viable solution. They'll often guess wrong.

If you don't want to name a number, ranges work: "We have between $10,000 and $20,000 allocated for this project." That's enough information to scope appropriately.

## One Last Thing

A brief is a starting point, not a contract. A good developer will ask follow-up questions, challenge assumptions, and suggest alternatives you haven't considered. That dialogue is where projects get shaped into what they should be.

But you can't have a productive dialogue if one party has no idea what the other is trying to build. A clear brief starts that conversation in the right place.
