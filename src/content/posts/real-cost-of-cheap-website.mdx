Every month, a business owner contacts us to rebuild a website they paid for 18 months ago. The story is almost always the same: they got a quote from someone much cheaper, the site looked fine at first, and then the problems started.

We're not writing this to disparage competitors. We're writing it because the pattern is real, the costs are significant, and most of it is avoidable with a better evaluation framework.

## The Actual Costs of a "Cheap" Website

### Cost 1: The Rebuild

The most direct cost. A site built without proper architecture, documentation, or maintainability in mind often can't be improved incrementally — it has to be thrown away and rebuilt. When you add the cost of the original build to the cost of the rebuild, plus the lost revenue during the period the site underperformed, the "cheap" option frequently costs more than a quality build would have from the start.

We see this most often with sites built on page builders (Elementor, Divi, WPBakery) that have been heavily customised. The customisations create dependencies that make updates risky, migrations difficult, and performance improvements near-impossible without starting over.

### Cost 2: Security Incidents

WordPress powers roughly 43% of the web and is the most attacked CMS on the internet. A poorly maintained WordPress site — outdated plugins, no security hardening, no monitoring — is not a question of if it gets compromised, but when.

The cost of a security incident includes:
- Recovery and cleanup (typically $500–2,000 for a professional)
- Downtime while the site is offline
- Google blacklisting (if malware is distributed to your visitors)
- Customer trust damage if data is exposed
- Potential GDPR/data protection penalties if customer data is involved

A site maintained by someone who updates plugins irregularly and doesn't monitor for vulnerabilities is a liability.

### Cost 3: Invisible SEO Damage

Bad technical SEO is invisible until you look for it. Common issues in cheap builds:

**Slow page speed.** Google uses page speed as a ranking factor. A site that loads in 8 seconds on mobile ranks below a competitor that loads in 2 seconds, everything else being equal.

**Duplicate content.** A poorly configured WordPress site often generates multiple URLs for the same content — the canonical URL, the category archive URL, paginated versions — which splits "link equity" and confuses search engines.

**Missing structured data.** Schema markup helps search engines understand what your site is about and can enable rich results (star ratings, FAQs, etc.) in search listings. It's rarely included in cheap builds.

**Broken internal linking.** If your site's navigation and internal link structure doesn't reflect what you want to rank for, you're leaving organic search potential on the table.

None of these show up obviously when you look at a website. You need technical SEO tools to spot them, and most business owners don't look until they notice they're not ranking.

### Cost 4: Ongoing Developer Dependency

A site with no documentation, no clear code structure, and no handover process means every future change requires the original developer. When that developer is unavailable, unresponsive, or out of business — which happens — you're stuck.

We regularly receive requests to take over sites from developers who've disappeared. In the worst cases, the site is on a hosting account only the original developer has access to, the code is unreadable, and there's no backup. The only option is a rebuild.

## How to Evaluate a Website Proposal

### Look at Their Own Site

A web development company's website is their own product. If it loads slowly, looks dated, or has obvious design or usability issues, that's information. Run their site through Google PageSpeed Insights. If it scores below 70 on mobile, ask them about it.

### Ask Specific Questions

**"What CMS or framework will you build this on, and why?"**
There's no single right answer, but there should be a reasoned answer. "We use WordPress for everything" is different from "we recommend WordPress for this project because your content team needs to update it regularly and we'll set it up with a clean custom theme, not a page builder."

**"How will the site be maintained after launch?"**
Updates, backups, uptime monitoring, security scanning — who does these and how? If the answer is vague, that's a red flag.

**"Who owns the hosting account and domain?"**
You should always own both. Always. If a developer insists on controlling your hosting, walk away.

**"Can I speak to a previous client?"**
Reputable agencies can provide references. Reluctance to do so is informative.

**"What happens if I want to switch developers in the future?"**
A good developer builds sites that can be handed off cleanly. They should be able to tell you what documentation, access, and handover they provide.

### Understand What's Included

Get itemised quotes. "A website for $1,500" tells you nothing. What's included?

- Custom design or a template?
- How many pages?
- Does it include copywriting or will you provide content?
- Is hosting included, and for how long?
- What's the support arrangement after launch?
- Are revisions included during development, and how many?

The difference between a $1,500 quote and a $5,000 quote is often that the first one doesn't include things you'll need.

## The Right Framework

The right question isn't "how do I get the cheapest website?" It's "what's the minimum investment that delivers a website that achieves my business goals reliably over the next three to five years?"

For a simple five-page marketing site for a local business, that might be $3,000–5,000 from a quality developer. For an e-commerce platform, $8,000–20,000+. The specific numbers vary, but the principle doesn't: you're paying for reliability, maintainability, and outcomes — not just pixels on a screen.
